The Old Beer Hall in the Bronx

By Keith Williams

  • Aug. 2, 2018

Q. I remember an abandoned multi-story wood building at Boston Post Road (U.S. Rte. 1) and Dyre Avenue in the Bronx. I was told that it was a “beer garden.” What’s the story here?

A. It was indeed a beer hall, which was located at 4018 Boston Road (a similar road with post-Revolutionary War origins) in Eastchester. The building was torn down in 1960.

Various posts at that location had quenched the thirsts of travelers between New York and Boston since the early 19th century. The history of this spot possibly extends even earlier: a checkpoint was set up close to this site in 1673, when the Dutch briefly retook New Netherland from the English. Around this time, it also could have served as a sort of tavern, according to the book, “The Old Boston Post Road,” by Stephen Jenkins.

The first post-colonial tavern was housed in a converted doctor’s residence. The owners, the Armstrong family, were known for serving a good meal—including, allegedly, to the Marquis de Lafayette, who is said to have stopped here en route to Boston in 1824. (A contemporary account, however, places his meal in New Rochelle, a few miles up the road.)

Starting in the late 19th century, the Bronx became a haven for German-Americans escaping the tenement slums of the Lower East Side. Seeing an opportunity, Henry Dickert purchased the hotel in 1900 and expanded the property significantly, rebranding it as the Old Point Comfort Hotel and Park.

In an advertisement, Dickert boasted of his park’s numerous amenities, including “Swings, Carousel, Long Distance Rifle Range, large Dancing Pavilion in central location of Park, 4 Bowling Alleys, Music Pavilion; Playgrounds for young and old,” with space for as many as 5,000 patrons on the four-acre site.

“Real German cooking prevails,” the brochure added, which revelers could wash down with authentic pilsners.

For seven cents, patrons could take the New York, Westchester & Boston Railway — a small part of which survives today as the 5 train — to the Dyre Avenue stop, just steps from the park.

Those wishing to hold private parties had to agree to certain rules, including one stating, “The dancing of two gents together is strictly prohibited; also not allowed to dance with coats off.”

The area’s geography was much different at the time, too. The garden was located near Holler’s Pond, which was fed by a narrow waterway called Rattlesnake Brook. As the expanding city began to devour the surrounding farmland, the pond was filled in and built over in the early 1950s, while most of the brook now flows through underground pipes.

In later years, the park was also called Breinlinger’s, after its owner, Kilian Breinlinger, who purchased the property from Dickert in 1926. The park closed in 1957.
