Summer in the City

Things to do This Weekend: Free Arts in Parks!

By Margot Boyer-Dry and Tejal Rao

  • Aug. 2, 2018

This week we’re doing what New York summer does best: bringing the culture outside.

But these plans don’t start until tomorrow, so please take the time now to take our survey here, or sign up here for a chance to win a Governors Island camping trip.

And as always, forward this to your friends, read up on our other events and write us with suggestions. We’re almost to Labor Day, people!

-Margot and Tejal

Site-Specific Dance Festival on the Queens Waterfront

Margot, What’s the Plan?

You’ve learned by now that I always check things out before making any recommendations. And what I saw last week, a preview of the INSITU dance festival, was very New York and very cool, especially with its use of public space.

I found myself at the volleyball court in Hunter’s Point South Park to watch two contemporary dancers fling each other in and out of the sand, crumpling, rising and wrestling in a dance performance they had choreographed themselves. Though it was just a rehearsal, the art was moving — not just to me, but to other parkgoers, who whooped and applauded as they passed by. One woman stopped to record the dance on her phone.

That’s the power of public spectacle. It draws you in, fully present, to share a fleeting moment with whomever else happens to be nearby. Then you have the story of your day.

That experience is the foundation of the festival, happening this Saturday and Sunday, when roughly 20 dance troupes perform newly commissioned works at spots along the Queens waterfront.

All the pieces have been choreographed specifically for their locations, whether Hunter’s Point South, Gantry Plaza State Park, Queensbridge or Socrates Sculpture Park. That means that stairs, basketball courts, playgrounds and even the East River play a role in the movement of the dancers and the narrative of the performances. (If this sounds aggressively avant-garde to you, other groups will specialize in hip-hop, vogueing and African dance.)

Each park hosts a series of 10-minute dance works, repeating four times per day, at 1 p.m., 3 p.m., 5 p.m. and 7 p.m. With a dance workshop for the audience between each set of performances, you can spend a little over an hour in each area before you progress to the next to see something new.

Wherever you are, make friends; the festival is meant to draw all different kinds of New Yorkers into shared public space to interact and engage.

Tejal’s Before and After: Restaurants

If you’re getting a little thirsty between those 5 p.m. and 7 p.m. performances, wander over to M. Wells Steakhouse for their happy hour special: A bracing, icy cold, one-dollar martini accompanies every order of a half-dozen oysters. Want to stay for dinner? You really should.

Out on the waterfront, you can catch whiffs of the smoked meat from John Brown Smokehouse, a bare-bones, Kansas City-style barbecue restaurant serving burned ends, Reubens and pulled-pork-and-French-fry sandwiches.

For pressed sushi and Kyoto-style fare inspired by home cooking, visit Hibino. You can order the simple fresh tofu, which is seasoned with grated ginger and a syrupy sauce of soy and dashi, chilled.

Tejal’s Before and After: Bars

Rockaway Brewing Company’s taproom is open, close to the water and pouring dry stouts and loads more, including an in-season, low-alcohol lager brewed with corn tortillas, chiles and limes.

It’s a quick walk to the old neighborhood spot L.I.C. Bar, with its reliably unfussy drinks and chill patio. And if you’ve got family with you, Alewife is an unusually kid-friendly bar with beers and cider on tap — and a kitchen that happily serves chicken tenders.

Nearby

• While we’re on site-specific installations, check out the SculptureCenter’s “Convene,” a public sculpture created from aluminum canoes and painted to look like national flags. The temporary art exhibit aims to provoke questions about the makeup of a community’s identity (we are in Queens, remember).

• If you need some air conditioning or just some inside time, duck into the Noguchi Museum across from Socrates Sculpture Park. (See Culture Pass for free tickets to the Noguchi and SculptureCenter with your New York library card.)

• Coffee options include the very convenient COFFEED at Hunters Point South, and Communitea, a cafe with a “Saved by the Bell” vibe, cultivated by red neon and a checkered floor.

Required Reading

• Hear an interview with INSITU’s creator, Svea Schneider, on an episode of “Art VS Life” called “Dance Dance Evolution!”

• And, as recommended by Svea, watch this video of “bodies in urban spaces” and how they enthrall the public.

• Follow @heididuckler on Instagram. She’s a prominent site-specific choreographer and her videos are mesmerizing.

Noname at BRIC Celebrate Brooklyn, Prospect Park

Margot, What’s the Plan?

They say 2018 is the year of the female rapper, and I think “they” are spot on. While Cardi B is staking out territory on the charts, Tierra Whack, Princess Nokia, Yaeji and many others are producing exciting new work.

So it’s no wonder that the poet-rapper Noname is considered one of the crown jewels of this year’s BRIC Celebrate Brooklyn lineup, playing the Prospect Park Bandshell this Friday night.

Originally from Chicago, Fatimah Warner — who records as Noname — first dipped into music with appearances on tracks with her friends Chance the Rapper, Saba, Kirk Knight’ and Mick Jenkins. Then in 2016, Noname made, well, a name for herself, releasing “Telefone,” a hip-hop album of her life told through a series of phone conversations.

She’s known for her ambling, conversational flow and for kicking around heavy subjects like abortion and gun violence in a light, almost childlike voice, often over gospel-driven chord progressions.

If we’re lucky, maybe we’ll hear some new tracks off her forthcoming album, “Room 25.”

So bring along your friends, a picnic and blankets to spread in the back (you can always approach the stage on foot if you want a closer view). The one thing you’ll want to leave to the venue is alcohol, since you’re not allowed to bring your own.

You’re about to catch one of the year’s most exciting acts, for free, under a canopy of leaves strung with tiny lights. Yep, that’s why you live here. Or at least, that’s why I do. See you there.

Doors are open at 6:30 p.m., with Topaz Jones and Jazze Belle opening. A $5 donation is suggested but not required.

We have BRIC Arts Media to thank for the appearance of Noname and the other free, big-name shows this summer. The nonprofit has organized BRIC Celebrate Brooklyn for the past 40 years, but this year the festival ends Aug. 11, so fit in your shows now!

Tejal’s Before and After: Restaurants

Want to eat outside? As a special for the show, the Double Windsor is packing up easy-to-carry boxes of fried chicken for two (thighs only — no bones!) with pasta salad, greens and parker house rolls.

Or close by, at East Wind Snack Shop, juicy pork dumplings are the draw. The kitchen grinds meat in-house, folds dumplings by hand and pan-fries them so that one side is golden and crisp. To drink, there’s bubble tea made from freshly brewed oolong and mango purée.

A sit-down option is Talde. The restaurant on Seventh Avenue is especially chaotic and fun for a late brunch — when you can get the roti with smoked salmon and cream cheese and the noodle broth that tastes like buttered toast. The vegetarian options, and there are a few, don’t feel like an afterthought.

Tejal’s Before and After: Bars

Who cares how late your friends are when you’re in a shady spot with a cold grapefruit shandy and a warm pretzel? With a sprawl of a garden and big communal tables, Greenwood Park is an ideal meeting point for a big group — before or after the show.

The Owl Farm is a relaxed and dreamy place to lounge around, with its organic ciders from Brittany, canned sour beers and an excellent, summery draft list. Though if what you’re looking for are pitchers of Bloody Marys before you head to the park, noses tingling with wasabi, head to nearby Mission Dolores on Fourth Avenue.

Nearby

• If you arrive before the rest of your crew, stop by Terrace Books just outside the park and pick up a read for your extra time in the grass. Owned by a Park Slope institution, Community Bookstore, they have a mix of new, used and rare editions.

• Bring your post-show energy to Barbès, a neighborhood haunt that spins up regular dance parties around global music talent in its tiny back room. The genre-bending, Colombian-inflected band Yotoco plays Friday at 10 p.m.

• But if home is where you’re headed, the post-show pilgrimage to the closest 2/3 and B/Q stations is its own small event. Follow the crowds up Prospect Park West and scope its beautiful architecture under the soft glow of street lamps. (The F/G is in the other direction at 15th Street, a shorter walk but still impressive.)

Required Reading

• Whiz through this eight-minute episode of Josh Rutner’s “Album of the Week,” all about Noname’s origin story in Chicago. Then watch the footage of her NPR Tiny Desk Concert.

• Another great way to get to know an artist is to read annotations of their work on Genius. Read through the pages on Noname’s “Diddy Bop” and “Shadow Man” as you listen.

• An episode of “The Bowery Boys” explains Prospect Park’s role in preventing Brooklyn from becoming a Manhattan suburb. While you listen, check out this illustrated slide show to see previous iterations of the park we know and love.

Tip Line

Extra, Extra

• Today is National Ice Cream Sandwich Day, and from noon to 2 p.m., Sony is handing out free Ben & Jerry’s sandwiches at its showroom in Madison Square Park (supply is limited, so hurry).

• Summer Streets start this weekend! For the next three Saturdays until 1 p.m., miles of city streets (mostly Park Avenue) become pedestrian zones for walking, running, biking and playing. See here for free cultural activities along the way.

• Lincoln Center’s Out of Doors is underway with three weeks of free, very exciting cultural programming in Damrosch Park. This week catch an Afropop party, Yoruba music and dance and more.

• The Coney Island Music Festival is Saturday at Surf and Stillwell Avenues. Free and very kid-friendly.

• On Sunday, mead makers unite for Mead Day at Honey’s in Bushwick, offering free honey wine workshops and tastings starting at 2 p.m.
